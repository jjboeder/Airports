1. Getting Started

The European Airports Map is a browser-based flight planning tool for VFR and IFR operations across Europe, built around the Diamond DA62. It works as a Progressive Web App — you can install it on your phone, tablet, or computer for quick access.

When you first open the app, you'll see an interactive map of Europe with nearly 3,000 airports shown as color-coded dots. Here's what you can do right away:

  • Tap any airport to see its details: weather, runways, frequencies, charts, performance data, and more
  • Search by ICAO code (EFHK), IATA code (HEL), or airport name (Helsinki) using the search bar at the top left
  • Check the weather — airport dots are automatically color-coded by flight category (green = VFR, blue = marginal, red = IFR)
  • Plan a route by opening the DA62 panel on the right side and clicking airports on the map
  • Tap anywhere on the map to see the local weather at that spot
Important: This tool is for planning assistance only. Always verify data against official sources (AIP, official MET reports, NOTAMs) before flight.

2.5 LLF (Low Level Forecast)

The LLF overlay shows forecast weather conditions for 10 Nordic areas (Finland, Sweden, Denmark, Estonia). When active, time step buttons let you move through the forecast periods. Click any zone to see:

  • Flight category with color-coded crosses for general and local conditions
  • Visibility and cloud base ranges
  • Present weather, icing layers, freezing level, cloud tops
  • Wind barbs for each area
  • Overview text (Finnish areas)

2.6 R/D/P Areas (Airspace)

The R/D/P overlay shows Restricted (R), Danger (D), and Prohibited (P) airspace zones when you zoom to level 7 or closer:

  • Solid borders and fill — the area is confirmed active (via schedule or NOTAM)
  • Faint dashed borders — potentially active (H24 or unknown schedule)
  • Click any area to see its type, name, vertical limits, validity, schedule, and frequencies
  • The system automatically cross-references NOTAMs to identify NOTAM-activated areas
When you're in route mode, clicking an R/D/P area adds a waypoint there instead of showing area details.

The search bar at the top left lets you find airports by ICAO code, IATA code, or name. Start typing (at least 2 characters) and matching airports appear. Click a result to fly to that airport and open its popup.

5. Performance Calculator

The Perf tab in each airport popup calculates DA62 takeoff and landing performance using data from the AFM (Doc. No. 11.01.05-E). It automatically uses live METAR data for temperature, QNH, and wind — or lets you enter values manually when no METAR is available.

5.1 What You See

  • Atmosphere line: pressure altitude, OAT, density altitude, and QNH
  • Weight input: adjustable aircraft weight (defaults to W&B ramp weight or 2,300 kg)
  • Runway performance table for each runway end:
    • TO 50ft — takeoff distance to clear a 50-foot obstacle
    • LDG 50ft — landing distance from a 50-foot obstacle
    • TO margin — available runway minus takeoff distance (green = OK, red = insufficient)
    • LDG margin — available runway minus landing distance
    • Climb gradient over ground (yellow if below 3.3%)
    • BEST badge on the runway end with the most takeoff margin
    • Detail row: headwind/tailwind, crosswind, surface correction factor, rate of climb
  • Single Engine (OEI): single-engine ceiling (MSL) and rate of climb at field elevation. Shown in red when ceiling is below 5,000 ft or rate of climb is zero or negative.

5.2 How Calculations Work

Performance is interpolated from AFM tables using weight, pressure altitude, and OAT. Wind corrections follow AFM guidance: headwind reduces distances, tailwind increases them. Unpaved surfaces (grass, gravel, etc.) apply a correction factor. The OEI ceiling is calculated from AFM single-engine climb data at the current conditions.

How to check performance: Open any airport popup and click the Perf tab. The calculations appear automatically using live METAR. Adjust the weight slider to match your planned weight. If no METAR is available, enter OAT and QNH manually.

6. Flight Categories

Every airport is color-coded based on its current ceiling and visibility. The app uses five categories:

BIR (Below IFR) is an extra category between MVFR and IFR. It gives you finer detail when evaluating approach minima at European airports.

The worst-case rule applies: if either ceiling or visibility puts the airport in a lower category, the lower one is shown. When skies are clear (no BKN/OVC/VV layers), ceiling is treated as unlimited.

7.2 Warning Badges

  • Wind badge (flowing lines): appears when wind > 15 kt or gusts > 20 kt
  • Ice badge (crystal): appears when icing conditions are detected (see Section 8)

7.3 Wind Shear Detection

The TAF timeline automatically detects wind shear between base conditions and TEMPO/PROB groups. A wind shear warning appears when:

  • Wind speed changes by 10 kt or more
  • Wind direction shifts by 60° or more (with at least 8 kt on both sides)
  • Gusts exceed sustained wind by 15 kt or more

7.4 Runway Wind Analysis

When METAR data is available, the runway table in the Info tab shows wind components for each runway end:

  • Head/Tail: headwind or tailwind in knots
  • Xwind: crosswind with direction (L/R) and gust crosswind
  • BEST badge: runway end with the strongest headwind
  • WORST badge: runway end with the strongest tailwind
  • Crosswind turns yellow above 10 kt and red above 15 kt

8. Icing Detection

The app checks for icing risk in three ways: from METARs (ground conditions), from TAFs (forecast), and from pressure-level data (at altitude).

8.1 Ground-Level Icing (METAR)

ConditionWhat triggers it
Freezing precipitationAny FZ weather code (FZRA, FZDZ, etc.) — always triggers
Precipitation in icing rangeTemperature −20°C to +2°C with any precipitation
Visible moistureTemperature −20°C to +2°C, ceiling ≤ 5,000 ft, and temp/dewpoint spread ≤ 3°C

8.2 Forecast Icing (TAF)

Uses the same logic but with TAF weather codes and current METAR temperature as a proxy. Shown in the TAF timeline ICE row and on route waypoint badges.

8.3 Altitude Icing (Pressure Levels)

Used in airgram charts and route icing warnings. Checks conditions at 8 pressure levels from 1000 to 450 hPa. Icing is flagged when temperature is −20°C to +2°C AND cloud cover > 50% or relative humidity > 80%.

8.4 Route Icing Warnings

The route planner checks for icing in three phases:

  1. Climb — checks all levels between airport elevation and cruise FL at departure
  2. Cruise — checks conditions at cruise FL along the entire route
  3. Descent — checks all levels between cruise FL and destination elevation

9. NOTAMs

9.1 Automatic Classification

NOTAMs are automatically sorted into critical and normal. Critical NOTAMs include airport or runway closures (CLSD), restrictions, danger areas, and unserviceable navaids (ILS, VOR, NDB, etc.).

9.2 Where NOTAMs Appear

  • Airport popup header: a badge next to the airport name (red if critical, amber otherwise)
  • NOTAMs tab: full list with critical NOTAMs highlighted in red
  • Route planner: NOTAM badge (N) next to each waypoint's flight category

11. Route Planning

11.1 Creating a Route

How to plan a flight:
  1. Open the DA62 panel (aircraft icon on the right side) and go to the Route tab.
  2. Click Start Route.
  3. Click airports on the map to add them as waypoints. You can also click open map areas to add coordinate waypoints (they'll be labeled with the nearest place name).
  4. Set your fuel load, power setting, departure time, and flight level in the route settings.
  5. The route summary updates automatically with distances, times, fuel, and weather.

11.4 Managing Waypoints

  • Add: click airports or map locations while in route mode
  • Remove: click the × next to any waypoint
  • Drag: non-airport waypoints can be dragged on the map
  • Undo Last: removes the most recently added waypoint
  • Clear Route: removes all waypoints
  • Click a waypoint code to pan the map to that airport and open its popup

11.5 Alternate Airport

When you have 3 or more waypoints, the last one can be marked as an alternate by clicking the ALT toggle. The alternate leg is drawn as an orange dashed line, and fuel calculations split into Trip, Alternate, and Reserve (45-min IFR at selected power). A warning appears if fuel is insufficient.

11.6 Weather at Waypoints

When a departure time is set, each waypoint shows a flight category badge based on TAF data at the estimated arrival time. The system checks ±1 hour around arrival and uses the worst case. Wind and icing badges appear when relevant.

11.7 Auto Route (IFR)

Click Auto Route (IFR) to find an optimal IFR route between your first and last waypoints. It uses the Autorouter API and populates the route with IFR waypoints (intersections, VORs, NDBs). Toggle between fuel and time optimization by clicking again.

11.8 Runway Diagrams

At zoom level 11 and above, runway diagrams appear on the map for your departure and arrival airports, showing runway outlines, designator labels, and airport elevation.

12. Wind-Adjusted Calculations

When you set a departure time and flight level, the app fetches real wind data along the route and calculates accurate times and fuel.

12.1 How It Works

  1. 8–16 sample points are distributed evenly along the route
  2. Wind speed and direction at your flight level are interpolated from forecast pressure-level data
  3. Each leg's groundspeed is calculated: GS = TAS − headwind component
  4. Adjusted time and fuel are computed per leg

12.2 What You See

The "Wind adj" line in the route summary shows wind-adjusted time and fuel, average wind speed, average head/tailwind component, and the time difference versus the no-wind calculation.

13. Terrain & Icing Warnings

13.1 Terrain (AMA) Warnings

If your selected flight level is below the Absolute Minimum Altitude on any leg, you'll see a warning like:

⚠ FL080 is below terrain: EFHK → ESSA: AMA 9800 ft

13.2 OEI Ceiling Warnings

The route planner also checks whether your DA62 single-engine ceiling (at current weight and conditions) is above the AMA along each leg. If the OEI ceiling is below terrain, you'll see a warning showing both values — helpful for planning engine-out contingencies.

13.3 Route Icing Warnings

When icing is detected along the route (climb, cruise, or descent), a snowflake warning shows the affected segments and FL ranges.

13.4 On-Map Weather

When the Wx checkbox is on, wind barbs appear along the route showing wind speed and direction, cloud cover, and icing indicators at your flight level. Hover over any barb for detailed values.

14. Route Weather Panel

Click the Weather button in the route summary to open the floating Route Weather panel with four tabs:

14.1 Airgram

A weather cross-section along your entire route from surface to FL200. The X-axis shows distance with waypoint labels; the Y-axis shows altitude. Color, clouds, icing, and wind barbs follow the same conventions as the airport airgram.

14.2 GRAMET

An official GRAMET cross-section image from aviation weather services, showing turbulence, icing, and cloud layers. Click the image to open it full-size.

14.3 FL Compare

Compares wind conditions across multiple flight levels for your route — headwind/tailwind, groundspeed, time, and fuel. Helps you pick the most efficient altitude.

14.4 Briefing

An AI-generated route weather briefing that pulls together METAR, TAF, NOTAMs, and LLF data for all your waypoints into a plain-language summary covering departure, en-route, and arrival conditions.

Generates a Garmin-compatible .fpl file you can load into a Garmin GPS or flight planning software.

Generates a standard .gpx file compatible with most GPS devices and mapping apps.

Generates a comprehensive printable page including:

  • Route map with headings, distances, wind barbs, and R/D/P callouts
  • Runway diagrams for departure and arrival
  • Navigation log table with waypoints, magnetic headings, ETAs, fuel, and weather

Use the download and upload buttons in the DA62 panel header to save and restore all your settings (fuel, power, W&B inputs) as a JSON file.

17. How-To Guides

17.1 Plan a VFR Cross-Country Flight

  1. Open the DA62 panel and go to the Route tab. Click Start Route.
  2. Click your departure airport, then any intermediate stops, then your destination.
  3. Set your departure time (UTC) and flight level to enable wind calculations and weather at waypoints.
  4. Check the weather badges next to each waypoint — green means VFR, anything else warrants a closer look.
  5. Click the Weather button to open the route weather panel. Check the airgram for icing and clouds at your flight level.
  6. Use the FL Compare tab to find the best altitude for wind efficiency.
  7. Check the W&B tab to make sure you're within limits.
  8. Click the Perf tab on your departure and destination airports to verify runway performance.
  9. Review terrain warnings and OEI ceiling warnings in the route summary.
  10. Print your flight plan or export it to your GPS.

17.2 Check Weather Before Flying

  1. Open the app — METAR colors are already shown on all airports.
  2. Glance at the map: green dots mean good VFR, blue is marginal, orange to purple means reduced conditions.
  3. Click your departure and destination airports to see detailed METARs and TAFs.
  4. Turn on the Sat. Image or Radar overlay to see the big picture.
  5. Check the SWC button for the official significant weather chart.
  6. Click the Briefing tab in any airport popup for an AI-generated weather summary.

17.3 Evaluate an Unfamiliar Airport

  1. Search for the airport by ICAO code, IATA code, or name.
  2. Check the Info tab: ATC level, frequencies, runway dimensions, and surface type.
  3. Look for fuel availability, PPR requirements, and VFR/IFR status.
  4. Check the Perf tab — does the runway give you enough margin at your expected weight?
  5. Review the Charts tab for AIP approach plates and airport diagrams.
  6. Check NOTAMs for any closures or restrictions.
  7. Switch the basemap to Satellite to see the airport layout and surroundings.

17.4 Use the Performance Calculator for a Hot & High Airport

  1. Open the airport popup and click the Perf tab.
  2. Note the density altitude — it may be significantly higher than field elevation on a hot day.
  3. Check each runway end's TO 50ft and TO margin. Red means the runway isn't long enough.
  4. Try reducing weight using the weight input to find a safe operating weight.
  5. Check the Climb gradient column — anything below 3.3% is highlighted as a warning.
  6. Check the OEI line — if the single-engine ceiling is below surrounding terrain, you need a plan.

17.5 Check Airspace Along Your Route

  1. Turn on the Airspace overlay to see controlled airspace boundaries.
  2. Turn on R/D/P Areas to see restricted and danger zones. Solid fills mean active, dashed means potentially active.
  3. Click any R/D/P area to see its vertical limits, operating hours, and whether it's NOTAM-activated.
  4. Turn on Reporting Points to see VOR/NDB/DME navaids and VFR reporting points.
  5. When printing your flight plan, active R/D/P areas near your route are automatically included with callout boxes.
